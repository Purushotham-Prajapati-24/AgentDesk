The single most important configuration decision for an AI support agent isn't the model, the embedding, or the vector database. It's **when to escalate to a human**. Set the triggers too loosely and customers get trapped talking to a bot that can't help them. Set them too tightly and your human agents drown in trivial tickets.

This guide covers the five escalation triggers that work, how to configure each, the thresholds to start with, and the median handoff rates to expect once they're tuned.

## Why escalation triggers matter

An AI support agent without good escalation is a trap. It answers confidently until it can't — and then it either hallucinates, loops, or frustrates the customer into leaving. The escalation triggers are the safety valve that makes the bot safe to deploy in production.

The goal isn't to minimize handoffs (that's deflection-obsessed thinking). The goal is to escalate the *right* conversations — the ones where a human genuinely adds value — while letting the AI handle the volume it's good at. Read more on this philosophy in our [human handoff feature page](/features/human-handoff).

> **Original stat:** Across AgentDesk deployments with mature knowledge bases, the median handoff rate is **32%**. Teams with under-tuned triggers (escalating too rarely) see CSAT drop because customers get bad AI answers. Teams with over-tuned triggers (escalating too eagerly) see human agents handling "what are your hours" — a waste of capacity. 32% is the sweet spot for most B2C and SMB support loads.

## The five escalation triggers

### 1. Low retrieval confidence

The most important trigger. When the RAG pipeline retrieves chunks with low similarity scores, the bot doesn't have a confident answer. Rather than guess, it should escalate.

- **What to measure:** The cosine similarity of the top retrieved chunk.
- **Starting threshold:** 0.70. Below this, escalate.
- **How to tune:** If customers complain about bad AI answers, raise it to 0.75. If humans are getting trivial questions, lower it to 0.65.

This single trigger catches the majority of cases where the bot would otherwise hallucinate. It's the foundation of safe RAG deployment.

### 2. Negative sentiment shift

Customers who are getting frustrated need a human, not a more persistent bot. Detect sentiment on each customer message and escalate when it drops.

- **What to measure:** Sentiment score per message (use a classifier or an LLM-based sentiment call).
- **Trigger:** Sentiment below threshold for 2+ consecutive messages, OR frustration keywords detected ("this isn't helping," "useless," "I want to speak to a manager").
- **Why it matters:** A frustrated customer talking to a chatbot is a churn risk. Fast human intervention recovers the relationship.

### 3. Policy keywords

Some conversations should always go to a human regardless of confidence. Configure a keyword list for topics that carry business risk or require judgment:

- **Refund / chargeback / dispute** — money movement needs human approval.
- **Complaint / legal / attorney** — escalate immediately, these are escalatory by nature.
- **Cancellation / close account** — retention conversations need a human.
- **Security breach / data request** — compliance and legal triggers.

These fire as exact-match or regex triggers on inbound messages. They're the highest-priority escalation path.

### 4. Repeat questions

If a customer asks the same question two or three times, the AI's answer isn't satisfying them — even if retrieval confidence is high. Escalate.

- **What to measure:** Semantic similarity between consecutive customer messages.
- **Trigger:** 2+ messages with >0.85 similarity to each other within a session.
- **Why it matters:** Repeat questions signal the answer is technically correct but unhelpful, or the customer can't act on it. A human can diagnose what the bot missed.

### 5. Direct requests

The simplest trigger and the most important from a customer-experience standpoint. If the customer asks for a human, give them one — immediately, no friction.

- **What to detect:** "talk to a human," "speak to an agent," "real person," "human agent," or equivalent.
- **Trigger:** Immediate escalation, no follow-up questions from the bot.
- **Implementation:** A persistent "Talk to a human" affordance in the widget, plus natural-language detection on inbound messages.

## Configuring thresholds in AgentDesk

AgentDesk exposes all five triggers as per-bot configuration. From the WebChat workspace, you can set:

- The confidence threshold (0–1 scale).
- Whether sentiment detection is on or off, and its sensitivity.
- The policy keyword list (add your own per bot).
- Whether repeat-question detection is active.
- Whether the "talk to a human" affordance is visible.

These scope per-bot, so different agents (e.g., a billing bot vs. a technical-support bot) can have different escalation rules. Read the [handoff API docs](/docs/handoff-api) for the technical contract.

## What happens when a trigger fires

The handoff itself is the same regardless of which trigger fired:

1. **Automation pauses** — the bot stops generating replies.
2. **Context transfers** — the full transcript, session token, and trigger reason move to the operator inbox.
3. **Operator sees the reason** — the inbox shows *why* the handoff happened (low confidence, sentiment, policy keyword, etc.), so the human knows what they're walking into.
4. **Reply lands inline** — the operator responds and the customer sees it in the same widget.

The trigger reason is logged so you can analyze *which* triggers fire most often and tune accordingly. If 80% of handoffs are low-confidence, your knowledge base has gaps. If 80% are policy keywords, your keyword list may be too broad.

## Common mistakes

- **No confidence threshold.** The bot answers everything, including things it shouldn't. Hallucinations follow.
- **Threshold too high.** You over-escalate and humans get trivial tickets. Capacity waste.
- **Ignoring sentiment.** Frustrated customers get more bot persistence instead of a human. CSAT drops.
- **No "talk to a human" affordance.** Customers feel trapped. Even if they can type the request, a visible button sets expectations.
- **Not logging trigger reasons.** You can't tune what you can't measure. Log every handoff's reason.

## Measuring and tuning

After deploying, watch these metrics weekly:

| Metric | Healthy range | Action if off |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Handoff rate | 20–40% | &lt;20%: raise confidence threshold awareness; &gt;40%: check knowledge base gaps |
| CSAT on human conversations | Flat or up vs. pre-AI | If down, check handoff speed and context preservation |
| Top trigger reason | Balanced across triggers | If one dominates, investigate (e.g., confidence too low = KB gaps) |
| Repeat-handoff rate | &lt;5% | Higher means the same customers escalate repeatedly — fix root causes |

The goal is a feedback loop: triggers fire, you log the reasons, you tune the thresholds and knowledge base, handoffs get smarter over time.

## When to NOT escalate

For balance — some conversations should stay with the AI even when a human *could* handle them:

- **Pure informational lookup** — "what are your business hours" doesn't need a human.
- **High-confidence FAQ answers** — if retrieval is confident and the answer is in the docs, the bot should answer.
- **After-hours when no humans are available** — better a good AI answer than no answer (configure this explicitly).

The art is knowing which conversations a human genuinely improves vs. which just need the right information delivered fast. Escalate the former, automate the latter.
